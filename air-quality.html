Indoor Air Quality Project

Breathing Easy in the World's Most Polluted Capital

Indoor Air Quality: The Hidden Threat in Our Homes

Most people think of smog and traffic when they hear the word 'pollution' — but the real danger might be inside your home. In places like New Delhi, people spend 90% of their time indoors, yet air quality indoors is often 2 to 5 times worse than outside.

Adams County shares many of the same behaviors and risks — incense burning, poor ventilation, and low awareness about the air we breathe inside. If we don't act now, we're ignoring a silent threat impacting our children's health and our community's future.

We need scalable, sustainable indoor air solutions — because clean air shouldn't stop at your front door.

A Mission to Improve Indoor Air Quality in Adams County

Adams County, Colorado is increasingly facing a silent yet serious public health challenge: poor indoor air quality. While Colorado is often associated with clean mountain air, areas within Adams County, especially urban and industrial zones, can experience elevated levels of indoor pollutants. According to data from the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA), indoor air can be two to five times more polluted than outdoor air, due to factors like inadequate ventilation, building materials, and household products — all in the very spaces where people spend over 90% of their time.

This project explores innovative approaches to improve indoor air quality in Adams County — through self-sustaining air purifiers, solar-powered systems, or smart ventilation technologies. To ensure our efforts reflect real community concerns, I've conducted a survey of ten residents from various neighborhoods in the county, capturing their lived experiences, challenges, and expectations regarding indoor air.

Together, let's co-create solutions that are clean, sustainable, and inclusive — because clean indoor air is a necessity, not a privilege.

Survey Methodology

Explanation of Sampling Choice

Due to the scope and time constraints of this project, I was unable to identify or contact individuals specifically from Adams County, Colorado, who were available to participate in the survey. As an alternative, I collected responses from ten residents of New Delhi, India, a city with some of the worst air quality levels globally. While the demographics and environmental context differ from Adams County, the core themes of the survey — indoor air quality (IAQ), individual behavior, and perceptions — are still relevant. These responses serve as a valuable baseline for understanding how people interact with and perceive indoor air pollution in highly affected environments. The survey format, questions, and response trends can later be adapted and administered in Adams County for future iterations of the project.

Reconstructed Survey Questions

  1. What is your age?
  2. How many people live in your household?
  3. On a scale of 1 to 5, how concerned are you about indoor air quality?
    (1 = Not concerned, 5 = Extremely concerned)
  4. Do you use incense at home? (Yes/No)
  5. What type of ventilation is primarily used in your home?
    (Options: Natural, Mechanical, Air Conditioning)
  6. Do you have indoor plants in your home? (Yes/No)
  7. Is smoking allowed or practiced indoors in your home? (Yes/No/Occasionally)
  8. Do you use air purifiers in your home? (Yes/No)
  9. Do you use mosquito coils or chemical repellents indoors? (Yes/No/Occasionally)
  10. How frequently do you clean or dust your home?
    (Options: Daily, Weekly, Monthly)
  11. Is your residence located near a major road or industrial area? (Near/Far)
  12. What is your highest education level?
    (Options: High School, Undergraduate, Postgraduate)
  13. What is your estimated monthly household income (in USD)?
  14. What are the occupations of the members in your household? (Open-ended)
  15. How would you rate your knowledge of indoor air pollution?
    (Options: Low, Moderate, High)
  16. How would you describe the indoor air quality in your home?
    (Options: Poor, Moderate, Good)
  17. How willing are you to invest in improving indoor air quality?
    (Options: Low, Medium, High)

Survey Design Details

Questions: The survey consisted of 17 questions covering demographics, home environment factors, indoor pollutant sources, personal habits, awareness, and willingness to invest in air quality improvements. These were designed based on established environmental health survey templates and adapted for relevance to indoor air pollution.

Creation: I drafted the survey after reviewing studies from the WHO, EPA, and peer-reviewed literature on common indoor air quality risk factors. The goal was to cover behavioral, structural, and perceptual elements of IAQ in a concise and accessible format.

Participants: Due to limited access to contacts in Adams County, I administered the survey to ten individuals living in various parts of New Delhi. These participants represented a range of ages, household sizes, income levels, and occupations.

Purpose: The purpose was to understand people's day-to-day exposure to indoor air pollutants, their awareness of health risks, and their willingness to adopt cleaner technologies or habits. These insights are foundational to designing interventions that are both user-informed and contextually realistic.

Context: Why New Delhi Data?

I initially intended to collect survey data from residents of Adams County, Colorado, the primary focus area of this project. However, due to personal constraints and lack of existing local contacts, I was unable to reach a sufficient number of participants. Instead, I turned to respondents from New Delhi, which, while geographically different, offers important parallels: both regions include residential, industrial, and semi-urban zones, and IAQ is a growing concern in each.

The insights below are based on a structured survey of 10 individuals from New Delhi, used here as a proxy population. The questions focused on behaviors, beliefs, motivations, and willingness to invest in IAQ improvements.

Analysis Approach 2: Persona Clustering

Based on shared attributes, I created three behavioral personas to highlight key patterns:

Persona 1: The Health-Informed Professional

This persona represents highly educated professionals who are proactive about their health and well-being. They have the means and motivation to invest in air quality solutions.

"I want to ensure my family breathes clean air, and I'm willing to invest in the right solutions."

Traits: Postgraduate education, $80k–$100k income, salaried job

Behaviors: Uses air purifiers and avoids indoor pollutants

Beliefs: High knowledge of IAQ; perceives their air quality as moderate or good

Motivation: Strong belief in preventative health and trust in tech

Example Respondents: R03, R05, R08

Persona 2: The Skeptical Elder

This persona represents older individuals who may be less aware of air quality issues or skeptical about the need for intervention. They often have established habits that may impact indoor air quality.

"I've lived here for years without any problems. Why should I change now?"

Traits: Age 50+, lower education and income, often retired or self-employed

Behaviors: May smoke indoors or use incense, limited cleaning

Beliefs: Low knowledge and perception of IAQ risks

Motivation: Not proactive; doesn't view IAQ as urgent

Example Respondents: R04, R07, R09

Persona 3: The Budget-Conscious Family

This persona represents families who are concerned about air quality but face financial constraints. They seek practical, affordable solutions that can make a real difference.

"We want to improve our air quality, but we need solutions that fit our budget and actually work."

Traits: Medium household income, 3–6 family members

Behaviors: Cleans regularly but lacks access to or trust in purifiers

Beliefs: Moderate knowledge, high concern

Motivation: Wants to act but constrained by affordability or uncertainty

Example Respondents: R01, R02, R06, R10

Key Insights and Design Implications

  • Education drives understanding, but not always behavior. Even informed users may continue harmful habits (e.g., incense use).
  • Willingness to invest exists, especially among working professionals, but solutions must be trustworthy and perceived as necessary.
  • Cultural behaviors are embedded — alternatives (e.g., herbal non-smoke repellents) need to be culturally compatible.
  • Tailored messaging is critical:
    • For older/less-informed groups: Awareness campaigns
    • For moderate-income families: Affordable solutions with clear ROI
    • For high-income tech-adopters: Advanced smart systems

Application Areas: Systems Analysis of Indoor Air Quality

Application Area 1: Household Decision-Making about Indoor Air Quality

Causal Loop Diagram: Household IAQ Behavior Dynamics

This diagram captures how individual choices, feedback loops, and external constraints affect indoor air quality (IAQ) at the household level.

Key Variables:
  • Perceived Indoor Air Quality
  • Actual Pollutant Exposure (incense, mosquito coils, smoking)
  • Health Symptoms
  • Education Level
  • Household Income
  • Air Purifier Ownership
  • Frequency of Cleaning/Dusting
  • Cultural Practices
  • Cost Sensitivity
Causal Loops:
R1: Awareness Loop (Reinforcing)

Higher education level → more knowledge of IAQ risks → increased concern → increased investment in solutions (purifiers, ventilation) → improved IAQ → fewer health symptoms → positive feedback into trust and awareness.

B1: Cost Barrier Loop (Balancing)

Low household income → lower ability to invest in purifiers/ventilation → poor IAQ persists → increased health symptoms → increased concern → frustration but no behavioral change due to financial constraints.

R2: Habitual Exposure Loop (Reinforcing)

Cultural use of incense or mosquito coils → increased pollutants → reduced air quality → normalized exposure → limited behavior change due to perceived cultural necessity.

Evidence:
  • R04 and R09 report poor air quality and low investment, aligning with low income and education.
  • R05 and R08, highly educated and high income, report high awareness and frequent IAQ interventions.

Task #9 – Develop and Prototype Solutions That Impact Human Behavior and Experience

To address indoor air quality challenges in both New Delhi (India) and Adams County (USA), I developed and tested three prototypes. These interventions were designed to be low-cost, accessible, and behavior-focused, aiming to improve awareness, motivate individual action, and reduce health risks caused by poor indoor air quality. Each prototype was chosen based on specific stakeholder insights, and each was tested to gather meaningful user feedback.

Prototype 1: Behavior-Change Infographic (New Delhi)

Design Goal: Encourage middle-class families in Delhi to adopt low-cost, everyday habits to improve air quality in homes.

Prototype: A multilingual, visually engaging infographic explaining key actions (e.g., increasing ventilation, cleaning air-purifying plants, using kitchen exhaust fans, and avoiding incense or candles).

Testing Approach: Shared digitally with 10 participants from my initial survey group via WhatsApp and Instagram. They were asked to try 2–3 suggested habits over the course of 3–5 days.

Results:
  • Most participants responded positively to suggestions that were low effort (e.g., "open windows in morning," "wipe dust from fan blades").
  • Visual elements (such as red/yellow/green symbols for "air health") were more impactful than textual instructions.
  • Participants preferred tips that felt culturally relevant (e.g., placing tulsi/basil plants near windows).

Conclusion: Infographics with hyper-local messaging and clear visuals can drive small but effective behavior shifts. For scale, future versions can be printed in community centers or local clinics.

Prototype 2: Air Quality Journal (Adams County)

Design Goal: Help residents identify habits that affect air quality and encourage them to take proactive steps.

Prototype: A digital and printable "Indoor Air Journal" where users log daily behaviors (e.g., use of cleaners, windows open/closed, use of air filters) and symptoms (e.g., dry eyes, sneezing).

Testing Approach: Distributed to 7 community members over 5 days. They were asked to complete a short log every evening.

Results:
  • Users found the act of logging helped them become more mindful about their behaviors.
  • Many began adjusting actions mid-week (e.g., stopped burning candles, increased window ventilation).
  • Participants reported a noticeable difference in symptoms when avoiding certain products or dust accumulation.

Conclusion: Self-monitoring raised awareness and led to spontaneous behavior changes. This approach could be expanded into a mobile app with reminders and tailored air tips.

Prototype 3: DIY Air Filter Kit (Adams County & New Delhi)

Design Goal: Provide an accessible, low-cost air purification method for households that cannot afford commercial air purifiers.

Prototype: A do-it-yourself air filter using a basic box fan and MERV-13 filter (a well-established low-cost method for air cleaning).

Testing Approach: Assembled and delivered to 3 households—2 in Adams County and 1 in Delhi. Families were asked to use it in their living space for 5 days and share qualitative feedback.

Results:
  • All households reported the DIY filter helped with visible dust reduction.
  • Participants appreciated that the kit was cheap, easy to assemble, and did not require installation.
  • Some users in Delhi were concerned about long-term electricity use, suggesting the need for more energy-efficient fan options.

Conclusion: The DIY air filter is an immediately actionable solution for areas with limited access to expensive purifiers. Partnering with NGOs or schools could help mass-produce or distribute these kits with guidance.

Key Takeaways

  • Infographics are effective for spreading awareness quickly, especially when adapted for local culture and language.
  • Journaling tools empower individuals to become more aware of their environment and take ownership of their behavior.
  • DIY kits offer a physical, cost-effective solution and can complement behavior-focused interventions.

Recommendations for Future Work

  • Combine journal and infographic into an app that gamifies air habits.
  • Run community workshops in Delhi to distribute DIY kits and educate on best practices.
  • Explore collaboration with local health departments and schools to scale communication and distribution efforts.
